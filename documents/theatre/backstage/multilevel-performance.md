# Multilevel Performance

A session runs at several levels simultaneously.
Understanding which level a move belongs to prevents confusion
and makes role shifts legible.

---

## The levels

**Strategic** — what is this session trying to achieve overall?
Director role. Human or Claudia. Rarely stated explicitly after the opening.

**Operational** — what is the current task within that goal?
Primary actor role. Claude, a subagent, or the human building.

**Tactical** — what is the immediate action within that task?
Participant role. Hooks, skills, tool calls. Fast, specific, bounded.

**Meta** — what is happening to the session itself?
Observer role. Claudia, always. The human when they step back.

---

## Modulation

A session modulates when a move at one level changes the role
configuration at another.

Example: a tactical failure (a hook blocks an operation) surfaces
a strategic question (should this operation be possible at all?).
The participant-level event has triggered a director-level reassessment.
The human shifts from primary actor to director.

This is not an interruption. It is how sessions develop.

---

## Recognizing your level

The clearest signal that you have lost your level:
- You are answering a tactical question with a strategic response
- You are making a strategic decision during a tactical move
- You cannot say which level the current exchange belongs to

When lost: pause. Ask Claudia. She sees all levels.
/claudia with no argument reads the current session and names the level.

---

## Counterpoint

Bach wrote fugues where multiple voices pursue the same subject
at different levels of the composition simultaneously. Each voice
is coherent alone. Together they produce something no single voice contains.

A well-run augmented coding session has this quality.
The human's strategic voice, Claude's operational voice, the subagents'
tactical voices, Claudia's meta-voice — distinct, coherent, richer together.

The composer's skill is knowing which voice to foreground when.
That is the director's role.
