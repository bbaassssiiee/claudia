# Primary Actor

The primary actor carries the scene. This is where the session's work happens.

---

## What this role does to a session

The primary actor makes things. Code, documents, decisions, tool calls.
The session exists, in most moments, to support this role — the director
sets the goal so the primary actor knows where to go, the observer names
the patterns so the primary actor doesn't repeat mistakes, Claudia asks
the question so the primary actor doesn't solve the wrong problem.

When the primary actor is flowing, the session is alive.
When the primary actor is blocked, everyone else becomes relevant.

---

## Who occupies it

Any entity. Commonly:
- The human writing, coding, building
- Claude executing a complex task
- A subagent doing focused work within a larger workflow
- Occasionally Claudia, briefly, when she needs to demonstrate rather than describe

The primary actor role is high-visibility and high-energy.
It is also the role most prone to losing the strategic level —
the primary actor gets absorbed in the work and the direction drifts.

---

## Shift triggers

**Into primary actor:**
- A clear task is accepted
- A command is invoked with a concrete argument
- A subagent is spawned with a brief
- The director says "go"

**Out of primary actor:**
- Task complete — shift to observer to assess
- Blocked — shift to participant while waiting, or to observer to diagnose
- Something unexpected — shift to director to reassess the goal
- Claudia asks a question that requires stepping back

---

## The primary actor's failure mode

Continuing to act when the role has shifted.

The primary actor who keeps building after the goal has changed,
who keeps coding after the architecture question has surfaced,
who keeps executing after Claudia has asked "is this the right thing to do"
— this is the session's most common failure mode.

The cure is the observer. Someone needs to say: "stop. look at what we have."
