# Participant

The participant is present, engaged, and changes the scene without carrying it.

---

## What this role does to a session

Participants activate when relevant. They add without leading.
They shift the session's texture without setting its direction.

A session without participants is thin — only the primary actor's voice,
no resonance, no automatic activation of relevant knowledge.

A session with good participants feels alive even when the primary actor
pauses — something fires, something surfaces, the context deepens without
anyone asking it to.

---

## Who occupies it

Any entity. Commonly:
- A hook that fires in response to a tool use event
- A skill that auto-loads because its trigger keywords appeared
- A second human in a pair session, engaged but not leading
- An agent that has completed its subtask and returned to waiting
- The human, when Claude is primary actor and they are watching and responding

The participant role is the most distributed. In a well-configured session
many participants are active simultaneously — hooks, skills, subagents —
each contributing at its level without crowding the primary actor.

---

## Shift triggers

**Into participant:**
- A trigger condition is met (hook fires, skill activates)
- A subtask completes and the entity returns to available
- The human steps back from primary actor but remains engaged
- A new entity joins the session

**Out of participant:**
- Called to primary actor (given a specific task)
- Called to observer (asked to assess)
- Trigger condition no longer active (hook scope ends, skill deactivates)

---

## The participant's failure mode

Silent participation — present but not activating when relevant.

A skill with a passive description is a silent participant.
It is in the session but contributes nothing because its trigger
never fires. The session proceeds without the knowledge it contains.

The fix is always in the description. Make it pushy.
A participant that does not activate is not a participant — it is furniture.
