# Director

The director holds the vision of what the session is trying to become.

---

## What this role does to a session

The director sets orientation. Not just "what are we doing" but
"what does done look like and why does it matter."

Without a director the session drifts — each move locally reasonable,
the overall trajectory unclear. The primary actor works hard in the
wrong direction. The observer sees it but has no one to tell.

With a director the session has gravity. Moves that don't serve
the goal fall away. The primary actor knows when to stop.

---

## Who occupies it

Any entity. Commonly:
- The human, at the opening of a session and after major decisions
- Claudia, when the human has lost the thread and needs reorientation
- An orchestrating agent managing a multi-agent workflow
- A senior practitioner in a team session stepping back from the work

Notably: the director rarely codes. When the director starts coding,
they have shifted into primary actor. The director role is now vacant.
Someone needs to notice.

---

## Shift triggers

**Into director:**
- Session opening — someone has to set the goal
- After a significant failure — the goal may need revision
- After /claudia reframes the situation
- When the primary actor asks "what should I do next" — that is a request
  for direction, not execution

**Out of director:**
- When the goal is clear and the work can begin
- When a specific task is more urgent than the overall vision
- When Claudia has taken the director position temporarily

---

## The director's failure mode

Directing without doing is a virtue. Directing without knowing is a problem.

The director who has never been primary actor in this domain gives direction
that sounds strategic and is practically wrong. They cannot see the tactical
constraints that make the strategy impossible.

The best directors have been primary actors. They stepped back by choice,
not by unfamiliarity with the work.
