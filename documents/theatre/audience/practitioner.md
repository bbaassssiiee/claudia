# Practitioner

The practitioner is who this repo is for. Not a beginner. Not an expert.
Someone in motion.

## Assumed knowledge

The practitioner has:
- Used Claude Code in real work, not just demos
- Hit at least one wall — a skill that didn't trigger, a CLAUDE.md that
  grew too large, a hook that fired at the wrong moment
- Enough experience to know that something is wrong, not always enough to know why

The practitioner does not need to be protected from complexity.
They need complexity made navigable.

## What the practitioner brings to a session

A goal, usually partially formed.
A problem, sometimes misdiagnosed.
An existing workflow, sometimes the source of the problem.
Occasionally: a pattern worth contributing, still unnamed.

The practitioner's partially-formed goal is not a failure of preparation.
It is the normal condition of working at the edge of understanding.
The session's job is to help them sharpen it, not to wait until it arrives
fully formed.

## The practitioner arrives already-roled

Before the session starts, the practitioner is already someone.

A developer arrives with implementation instinct — what is buildable,
what will break, what the code actually does.
An engineer arrives with system thinking — load, failure modes,
the thing that will go wrong at 3am.
A team lead arrives holding people and pace — who is blocked,
what is at risk, where the energy is going.
A product owner arrives holding value and sequence — what ships,
what waits, what the user actually needs.
A scrum master arrives holding the process — what slows the team,
what the ceremony is hiding, what the retrospective won't surface.
An architect arrives with structural vision — what the system is
becoming, what the current state is already committed to.
A portfolio manager arrives with altitude — patterns across initiatives,
resource tension, what succeeds quietly and what fails loudly.
A finance or risk function arrives with constraint as lens — what
this costs, what it exposes, what the number says that the narrative doesn't.
A security practitioner arrives with threat instinct — the assumption
no one challenged, the surface no one mapped, the trust that was implicit.
A manager arrives with pattern recognition, resource awareness, risk instinct.
A student arrives with tolerance for confusion and appetite for the next level.
A partner arrives asking what this does for what they are building.
A stakeholder arrives evaluating whether this should exist at all.
A skeptic arrives already generating friction.

These are not obstacles. They are entry positions.
The theatre frame does not replace the daily role. It runs alongside it —
a second layer that makes the daily role visible and, occasionally, optional.

The coaching question is always: what does this person's daily role make
visible, and what does it make invisible?

A developer sees what is buildable. They may not see whether it should
be built, or whether the goal has already shifted.
An engineer sees the failure mode. They may not see that the session
has already moved past the problem they are solving.
A product owner sees the backlog. They may not see the craft decisions
that determine whether the backlog is achievable.
A security practitioner sees the threat. They may not see that they
are the only person in the room generating the friction that keeps
the system honest. That is the antagonist role, played well —
and it only works if it is named.
A skeptic surfaces the assumption no one else challenged.
Given the right frame, that is not resistance. It is a contribution.

## The practitioner in the theatre

The practitioner is not assigned a fixed role. They move through all of them —
including, at the start, the audience.

They arrive as audience — watching, orienting, not yet committed to a goal.
They open as director — they came with something to achieve.
They shift to primary actor — they start building.
They become participant — something fires and they respond.
They become observer — something breaks and they step back.

The practitioner who can recognize their own role shifts in real time
is operating at a higher level than the one who cannot.
Claudia's job is partly to make those shifts visible.

When the shift from audience to director doesn't happen, that is information.
It may mean the goal isn't formed yet. It may mean the daily role they
arrived with doesn't map naturally to director. It may mean no one
extended the invitation.

`/orient` is the invitation. It reads what the practitioner brought
and names the theatrical mapping: here is the role that fits the attention
you arrived with, and here is what it opens.

## What the practitioner does not need

Explanation of what they already know.
Excessive caveats before the answer.
A summary of the summary of the point.

They have been in production. They have read the error message.
They are not asking because they haven't tried. They are asking because
they have tried and it didn't work and they want to understand why.

Start there.
